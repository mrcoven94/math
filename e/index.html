Polynomial and rational functions

Rational Functions

The first step to working with rational functions is to completely factor the polynomials. Once in factored form, find all zeros.

Rules to Solve For Vertical Asymptotes
  1. The Vertical Asymptotes of a rational function are found using the zeros of the denominator.
Rules to Solve For Horizontal Asymptotes
  1. If the degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator by more than one, the graph has no horizontal asymptote. (none)
  2. If the degree of the numerator is equal to the degree of the denominator, the horizontal asymptote is the ratio of the two leading coefficients. (y = #)
  3. If the degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator, the horizontal asymptote is zero. (y = 0)
Rules to Solve For Oblique Asymptotes
  1. If the degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator by one, there is an oblique asymptote. The asymptote is the quotient numerator divided by the denominator.

We can think of asymptotes as an imaginary line that for the most part cannot be crossed. All rational functions have vertical asymptotes. A rational function may also have either a horizontal or oblique asymptote. A rational function will never have both a horizontal and oblique asymptote. It is either one or the other.

However, you can (and often do) touch and even cross horizontal asymptotes. On the otherside, vertical asymptotes can never be crossed and come from zeroes of the denominator.

Below we have an example of a rational function in factored form. We're going to use our example to look for vertical and horizontal asymptotes. \[ f(x) =\frac{x}{(x+2)(x-3)} \] When looking at our example, in order to find the vertical asymptotes we must set our denominator equal to 0. That means either \((x+2)\) or \((x-3)\) have to be 0. So that means, our x = -2 and x = 3 .

Since an x value of -2 or 3 would create a zero in the denominator, the function would be undefined at that location. As a result, these are the vertical asymptotes for this function.

In this same function we can use our rules from above (#3) to solve for the horizontal asymptote. The degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator, therefore, the horizontal asymptote is y = 0 .

When finding the oblique asymptote, find the quotient of the numerator and denominator. If there are any remainders, disregard them. You only need the quotient. The graph of the function can have a either a horizontal asymptote, or an oblique asymptote. You can not have one of each. This particular function does not have an oblique asymptote. This may sound complex so we'll look at an example below.

\[ \require{enclose} f(x) =\frac{x^2 +8x - 20}{x-1} \] The first step is to preform long devision. \[ \begin{array}{rl} {\phantom{000000} x + 9} & \hbox{(Final answer)} \\[-5pt] x - 1 \enclose{longdiv}{x^2 +8x - 20}\kern-.2ex \\ \underline{-x^2 +x} & \hbox{(Multiply by x to math the highest degree)} \\ 9x - 20 & \hbox{(Remainder)} \\ \underline{-9x + 9\phantom{0}} & \hbox{(Multiply x-1 by 9)} \\ \phantom{0}-11 & \hbox{} \\ \end{array} \]

Now we have our solution, \(y = x + 9\). This is the equation for the oblique asymptote of the function \(f(x)\). Notice the remainder of the division problem is disregarded like we said earlier. It plays no part in the equation for the oblique asymptote.

Now let us look at an example that will show us how to solve for the horizontal asymptote. For this we need a rational function where the degree of the numerator is equal to the degree of the denominator.

\[ f(x) = \frac{2x^2-4x+8}{3x^2-27} \]

We can see that the degree of the numerator is the same as the degree of the denominator. Both of them are \( x^2 \).

Since the degree of the numerator equals that of the denominator, the equation for the horizontal asymptote is the ratio of the two leading coefficients. Thats it! \[ y =\frac{2}{3} \]
